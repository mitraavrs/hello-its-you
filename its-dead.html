I’ve only ever seen peacocks from a distance. A glimpse of a few wild ones from the window of a moving train. A couple of them at a farm, a long time ago.

Even at a distance, their striking colors made them shine. They were alive.

This one seemed like it was sleeping at first. Amidst the damp grass it was laying on, it glowed like a beacon in the dark. I thought I saw a slight twitch.

And then I saw its eyes. White. Glazed over.

I clawed at the ground in front of it for what felt like hours. With bare hands and my torch clutched between my thighs, I knelt in front of its dead body as I dug its grave. Clumps of grass sticking to my hands. Wet dirt beneath my finger nails. I dragged the peacock’s corpse into the hole and buried it.

The rain felt like fire on my skin. I wanted to cry. I wanted to scream.

I walked back to the lighthouse with my clothes sticking to my skin, my hair in knots. It was only after I was sat in bed, soaking the sheets with the rainwater that I had dragged in that I realized.
